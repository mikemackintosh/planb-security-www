# The Harder Part of Leading: Selling Your Security Strategy Without Losing Your Soul

**You did the hard work. You built the roadmap. Now comes the part nobody warned you about.**

---

So you've identified your strategy. You know the tactics -- the project work that builds up to something bigger. You've mapped it all out. Great. Now what?

Now you have to sell it. And that, my friend, is where most security leaders either rise or quietly get replaced.

## Data Is Your Currency

You sell your strategy with data. Call them KPIs, OKRs, operational metrics -- whatever label fits your org. The point is you need numbers that tell a story. Volume of access requests processed. Time-to-remediation on incidents. Compliance posture over time. These are the things that keep the lights on, and they're also your proof that the team isn't just sitting around waiting for alerts to fire.

But here's the double-edged sword: the moment someone says "grant access faster," you're suddenly being incentivized to deprioritize security. That's a tension every security leader lives with.

Here's what I've seen work. Instead of fighting it, get smart about it. Build a risk-tiering model. If someone wants access to public or internal data, that's probably low risk -- maybe you can automate a tertiary review and move on. But the second they're asking for confidential or restricted data? That's medium risk at minimum. And administrative access to super-sensitive datasets? That's high risk, full stop.

Now you're spending your time where it matters. You ask the hard questions on the high-risk ones: Why do you need this? What's the business case? How long do you need it? Because just because you're getting access doesn't mean you keep it forever. Maybe they need to file a risk exception. Maybe they need VIP-level approvals on the ticket. Either way, you've built a smart defense -- if something goes sideways, you can show you did your due diligence.

## OKRs: Most People Do Them Wrong

I'll just say it -- a lot of security teams set terrible OKRs. If your key result is "achieve 100% compliance" or "prevent 100% of security events," you're doing it wrong.

Key results are not the goal. Key results are the journey. Your objective is the goal. Measure yourself on how well you're making progress, not on some aspirational absolute.

Think about it this way: if you're rolling out a new client library across your engineering org, your first key result isn't "100% adoption by end of year." Your first key result is "one repository onboards the new library within a month." One. That's measurable, it's time-bound, and it's your first step on the journey.

And here's the thing -- once you actually hit 100% on something, it's no longer a key result. It's a business KPI. It's a keep-the-lights-on metric. You've graduated it. Move on to the next challenge.

The step-up plan from week to month to quarter to year? Those milestones become your KRs naturally. Are you at 10% by end of week? 25% by end of month? 75% in six months? Now you have something real to track against.

## Commander's Intent: The Leadership Concept Nobody Talks About

One thing most people leave out of their planning is commander's intent. If you disappeared tomorrow, would your team know exactly where they need to be headed? Would they know the end goal well enough to make decisions without you?

That's commander's intent. Your vision is the long-term goal. Your objectives are the midterm goals. Your tactical work adds up to your key results. Everything is connected. And if it's disjointed where you are right now, take point and fix it. Because a team that can only function when you're in the room isn't a team -- it's a dependency.

As Jocko always taught us: detach from the situation. You were hired because you're an expert. You've demonstrated the ability to assess, inform, execute, and own. Now your job as a leader is to protect your people from the noise the business throws at them, and to sell the plan using relationships.

## Relationships Are the Whole Game

This is the most important skill any security professional can have. Full stop. You could be the best hacker, the best defender, the best incident responder on the planet. But if you can't build relationships and prove the value of your team, the business will find somebody who can -- even if they lack the technical chops.

Having worn a lot of hats over the years -- network engineer, web developer, front-end engineer, IT manager, security leader -- the biggest gift that gave me was learning how to speak the common language of different business units. That's how you build empathy. That's how you anticipate objections before they happen. When you're about to pitch something that changes someone else's priority list, you already know why they're going to say no, and you've got the counter-argument ready.

Here's a real scenario. You go to engineering and say, "We need to implement passwordless authentication. It's going to take six months." Engineering pushes back -- it'll throw off their timeline, break features, delay new releases. Fair enough. But you leave them with this: "The risk of not doing this means account takeovers keep compounding, and that costs the business X dollars per incident."

Then you pivot to the product team. Because every P-zero firefight engineering has to handle is a feature that doesn't ship. And maybe you loop in legal, who doesn't want to file an 8-K because of an SEC-reportable material cybersecurity event. Or the comms team, who doesn't want to draft breach notifications. Suddenly your passwordless project isn't just a security initiative -- it's protecting revenue, reducing support costs, and keeping the company out of regulatory crosshairs.

That's how you turn security from a cost center into a profit center.

## Playbooks Build Trust (and Basically Everything Else)

Good leaders write playbooks. You can educate without being one-on-one when a playbook exists. High risk access review? Playbook. Medium risk? Playbook. Incident response? Playbook. Breach notification? Playbook with templates for every communication, every form, every role and responsibility.

I'd even joke that a CISO can measure their success by the size of their playbook library. I'm kidding. Playbooks. Plural.

But seriously, playbooks build confidence. People grow trust in you when they see how well those playbooks are written and how well they're executed. And that trust? That's the foundation of every relationship with every business leader you need on your side.

Here's one of my favorite moves: instead of sending phishing emails just to reprimand people and make them feel terrible about using computers, flip the script. Have your red team run the phishing campaign, but don't tell anyone outside of security. When it gets detected, let the whole organization follow the playbook like it's real. It's a fire drill. Everyone practices muscle memory. The security team groups the reports, responds according to the playbook, and it looks phenomenal.

Then you collect the metrics. You report on the efficiency and effectiveness of your program. Your security awareness training is paying off. People are reporting suspicious emails through the right channels. The team is executing flawlessly. And suddenly everyone in the org has seen your team in action -- and they're impressed.

## The Payoff

When people see your vision, your ability to deliver, the journey you're charting for the company -- when they see that you speak their language because you've taken the time to understand their world -- when they see your growing library of playbooks and the team executing without you having to micromanage every decision -- that's when you've made it.

That's when you can sleep at night. That's the whole point.

## Actionable Takeaways

- **Build a risk-tiering model** for access requests so you spend your review time where it actually matters.
- **Fix your OKRs.** Key results measure the journey, not the destination. If it says "100%," it's probably wrong.
- **Establish commander's intent.** If you vanished tomorrow, your team should know exactly where they're headed.
- **Invest in relationships across business units.** Learn their language. Anticipate their objections. Have your counter-arguments ready before you walk in the room.
- **Turn security into a profit center** by framing initiatives in terms of business impact -- revenue protection, cost reduction, regulatory risk.
- **Write playbooks for everything.** Then use them in fire drills to build muscle memory and demonstrate your team's capability.
- **Run phishing drills as team exercises**, not gotcha moments. Collect the metrics, report on effectiveness, and build trust.
