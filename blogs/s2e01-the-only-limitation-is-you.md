# The Only Limitation Is You: Why Security Culture Starts With Your Own Mindset

**Stop going through the motions. If you want to change how your organization thinks about security, you have to change how *you* think about it first.**

---

A year ago, I got home from Black Hat, sat down in front of a terrible microphone with zero preparation, and just started talking. No setup, no plan, no production value whatsoever. Fast forward a full rotation around the sun, and Plan B Security has a real production setup, an incredible community, and people reaching out to me regularly -- not just to grow themselves, but to grow their careers, their teams, and the people around them. That's humbling, and I don't say that lightly.

But here's the thing that keeps coming up in those conversations: people want to be better at security, but they don't know how to *think* about being better at security. They're stuck in the motions -- collecting logs, patching systems, responding to alerts, blocking phishing emails. They're doing the work, but they're not feeling it. And that gap between doing and feeling? That's where everything falls apart.

## The Alarm Clock Problem

Your alarm goes off in the morning. You have three choices: hit snooze, hit stop, or get up. That's it. And only you can decide.

If you're dragging yourself to a job you don't care about, the only thing getting you out of bed is the need to pay bills. That's survival, not motivation. But when you find work that's genuinely self-fulfilling -- work that gives you a reason bigger than yourself -- suddenly getting up isn't a struggle. It's a choice you *want* to make.

There's a Japanese concept called **ikigai**, which roughly translates to "reason to live." It's this intersection of what the world needs, what you love, what you're good at, and what you can be rewarded for. Picture a Venn diagram where all four of those circles overlap. That sweet spot in the middle? That's ikigai.

A lot of folks trying to break into cybersecurity are missing one or two of those pieces. They don't have that self-fulfillment, and they don't understand the value they can actually bring. Let me be blunt: if you're in security for the money, you're doing it for the wrong reason. If you're doing it for appreciation or to be some kind of industry celebrity, same deal.

The best security practitioners I know? They're the quietest ones. They're floating behind the scenes, sharing their skills with the people around them. They don't show up on Crunchbase. They don't have massive Twitter followings. They definitely aren't posting hourly on LinkedIn. What sets them apart is they make everyone around them better -- sometimes just by being in the room and letting people absorb through proximity.

## The "So What?" Framework

Here's something that fundamentally changed how I approach security decisions: two words. **So what?**

When I was building the security team at Shutterstock, someone would come to me wanting to ingest certain data into a system. My gut reaction might be "absolutely not." But then I'd ask myself -- so what? What if that data *was* in there? What would actually happen?

That question forces you to step back and think about real business value instead of reflexive security theater. Were we violating a compliance requirement? A privacy or legal obligation? Maybe. But asking "so what?" gave us the *what*, and then we could articulate the actual risk: the fines, the threats, the exposure.

And here's the magic -- it turned every security conversation into a teachable moment. Instead of being the department of "no," we became the team that helped people understand *why*. Suddenly, people weren't just complying with security policies. They were actively looking for ways to improve security because they wanted to. It was their choice to be better.

That's the difference between snoozing the alarm and getting out of bed.

## The Worst Security People I've Met

Some of the worst security practitioners are the ones who believe the only secure system is one that can't be accessed. Sure, in a vacuum, that's technically true. But if nobody can use it, what's the point? What value does it bring to the business?

Security isn't about locking everything down until it's unusable. It's about enabling the business to operate safely. If you can't effectively communicate the importance of what you're doing to your colleagues and leadership, your impact will be limited no matter how robust your measures are.

And security isn't just the security team's job. It's not IT's job. It's not engineering's job. It's not legal's or compliance's or support's. It's everybody's responsibility. But writing that in a policy doesn't make it real. You can have the most beautifully crafted security policy in the world, but if there's no procedure for how to actually implement it, you've failed to communicate. Period.

## Building a Culture That Breathes Security

There's a saying that you become the sum of the five closest people around you. Hang out with sharp, motivated people, and you'll start thinking and acting like them. Hang out with people who aren't pushing forward, and your growth is going to stall.

Company culture works the same way. The more you put security thinking in front of people -- even when nobody's asking for it -- the more it becomes part of how everyone operates. Offer it as a gift. Make it a present to them so they can grow.

When I was at Shutterstock, I started doing lightning talks on Fridays. Quick little sessions about how to be more secure, how to build a strong password (back then, song lyrics were a great method -- and the fact that most sites didn't allow spaces or special characters in passwords told you everything you needed to know about their SQL injection situation). Those talks turned into open conversations. I was the only security person for a long time, but I built an ambassador program that had people across the organization actively *wanting* to drive security forward.

I worked with team leads to figure out what their teams actually needed, then partnered with specific individuals to make sure it got delivered. Slack security, Salesforce configurations, onboarding processes -- we baked security into all of it. We held the line, and we got better. Not because anyone was forced to, but because everyone understood that being secure meant doing the right thing for the right reasons.

## Three Things You Can Do This Week

I'm not going to leave you with just philosophy. Here's what I want you to actually do:

### 1. The Security Value Audit

Block two hours on your calendar. Look back at everything you worked on this week and ask yourself: what was the security value I added? Then ask -- so what? What if I hadn't done that? What alternatives could I have taken? Did I consider those alternatives before I started?

Take your findings to your team, your cross-functional partners, whoever needs to see this kind of thinking. Show them the depth of thought you're putting into security decisions. It's like back in seventh grade when my dyslexia was so bad I couldn't do algebra, so I wrote a PHP function to solve all the problems for me. When the teacher said "show your work," I programmed the function to print out every step along the way. Show your work.

### 2. The 30-Minute Daily Investment

Put 30 minutes aside each day. Beginning of the day, end of the day, lunch, after dinner -- whatever works. Spend it reading a security blog, listening to a podcast, taking an online course, or experimenting with a tool you've heard people talk about. Download it, configure it, try it.

Stop saying "I guess" and "I think" and "maybe." Test your assumptions like the scientific method. Get a hypothesis, validate it, and keep that constant drive to grow. The more you learn, the more confidence you'll have. The more confidence you have, the easier it is to sell security to the business. Because let's be honest -- businesses *can* survive without a security function. They can skate by, pay fines, rebrand if they have to. It's all band-aids and broken glass, but they can technically still walk even if their feet are bleeding. Your job is to show them there's a better way.

### 3. Celebrate Security-Conscious Behavior

Find one way to recognize someone doing the right thing this week. A shout-out in a team meeting. A message in a Slack channel. A thank-you note. Maybe you've got a tool like Bonusly -- use it.

When you see someone call out a private key pasted in Slack and remind the owner to rotate it, reward that. You don't have to give them money -- give them props. Let them feel appreciated. Once you get critical mass within your organization for thinking securely, everyone's going to want that satisfaction. Everyone's looking for that praise.

## The Bigger Picture

We're not just implementing controls or ticking boxes. We're building a culture of security that permeates everything we do. When you approach cybersecurity with the right mindset, compliance stops being a burden and starts being a natural byproduct. Legal requirements, compliance requirements, and just doing things right -- sometimes you get all three for free when you plan accordingly.

Antoine de Saint-Exupery said it better than I ever could: don't assign people tasks and work -- teach them to long for the endless immensity of the sea.

In cybersecurity, you have the power to inspire others to long for the immensity of a secure digital world. The only limitation is you.
